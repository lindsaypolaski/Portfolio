Sloping into Growth

    This study looks at how slope characteristics can influence canopy asymmetry in Pinus Ponderosa at the University of Colorado Colorado Springs Austin Bluffs site. 47 different trees were measured for canopy radius, height, crown height, diameter, slope angle, slope aspect, and crown asymmetry using the Crown Asymmetry Index (CAI13). With this, data was processed in RStudio using a correlation test to show the weak, non-significant relationship between slope angle (r= 0.08, p=0.59) and slope aspect (r=0.09, p=0.56) with crown asymmetry. However, with further research, an outlier in the data was heavily influencing the overall correlation results. Tree ID 76 had a large influence on the overall correlation. After removing tree ID 76, the correlation changes significantly (with a new R value of -0.12). This confirmed that slope does not influence canopy in ponderosa pines in the UCCS campus on Austin Bluffs. This proves that other factors (possible wind exposure, competition, or branch loss) may have a role in shaping the tree’s canopy.

From Desert to City

    This study examines how land cover change in Las Vegas, Nevada, from 1990 to 2020 has contributed to the increase of the Urban Heat Island (UHI) effect. Using multi-temporal Landsat imagery (Landsat 5, 7, and 8) and analysis conducted in TerrSet 2020, this research integrates unsupervised land cover classification, Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI), and thermal band data to evaluate urban expansion, vegetation loss, and surface temperature change over time. Results indicate substantial growth in residential and commercial land use, accompanied by a decline in vegetation and open desert areas. NDVI analysis revealed an overall reduction in vegetation between 1990 and 2001, with an apparent increase by 2020 likely influenced by sensor differences rather than actual ecological recovery. Thermal analysis showed a consistent rise in surface temperatures, with increases of approximately 31°F between 1990 and 2001 and an additional 13°F by 2020. Areas experiencing the most significant urban expansion also exhibited the highest temperature increases, supporting the relationship between impervious surfaces and heat retention. The results demonstrate a strong correlation between urban sprawl, vegetation loss, and increasing surface temperatures, confirming the role of land cover change in intensifying the UHI effect in arid environments. Despite limitations related to classification methods and temporal gaps in data, this study highlights the effectiveness of remote sensing techniques for analyzing long-term environmental change and provides insight for future urban planning and sustainability efforts.

Communication Through ArcGIS Story Maps

    This project explores how different types of urban land use, ranging from highly developed areas to natural green spaces, affect animal diversity within the Fountain Creek Watershed in Colorado Springs. Through field observations, species counts, and spatial analysis, I found that areas with more vegetation, connectivity, and lower human disturbance support greater biodiversity, while heavily developed spaces limit wildlife presence to only the most adaptable species. This project highlights the importance of sustainable urban planning that integrates green spaces and protects natural habitats. By showing how biodiversity responds to land use, it emphasizes the need for cities to balance development with ecological health. Promoting connected, diverse green spaces not only supports wildlife but also enhances ecosystem services and human well-being, contributing to a more sustainable and resilient urban environment.

Communication Through Visuals

How GIS is Used in Emergency Management Careers

    PowerPoint presentations are important visual tools because they help organize information in a clear, structured, and engaging way. Instead of relying only on spoken words, PowerPoints allow presenters to support their message with visuals such as images, charts, graphs, and keywords. This combination of visual and verbal communication improves audience understanding and makes complex information easier to process. Many people learn better through visual aids, so PowerPoints can increase comprehension and retention by reinforcing key ideas in multiple ways. Another reason PowerPoints are important is that they help keep both the speaker and the audience focused. For the speaker, slides act as a guide to ensure that main points are covered in a logical order without forgetting important details. For the audience, slides provide a roadmap of the presentation, making it easier to follow along and understand how ideas connect. This structure is especially useful when presenting data-heavy or technical topics, where too much spoken information alone could become overwhelming.

Maps

    The intended purpose of my posters is to visually communicate spatial patterns of land cover change and environmental impact in an easy-to-understand format. Unlike text or numerical data alone, a map allows viewers to quickly identify where trends are occurring and how they are distributed across a region. In a presentation setting, this type of visual aid is especially effective because it helps the audience grasp complex geographic information at a glance, making it easier to follow key points about important roads, vegetation loss, temperature variation, and flood planes.

    When designing the maps, I focused on several important visual elements, including color, alignment, and clarity. Color choice was especially important, as I used intuitive color schemes (such as reds for higher heat or danger zones) to make the data more readable. I also ensured that the legend, title, and map elements were clearly aligned and spaced to avoid clutter. Simplicity was a key priority, as too much information can overwhelm the viewer and reduce the effectiveness of the map.

    I created these visual aids using GIS software, specifically ArcGIS Pro and used RStudio for the correlation charts. One major advantage of this tool is its ability to handle complex spatial data and produce highly detailed and accurate maps. It also offers strong customization options for symbology and layout. However, a limitation of ArcGIS Pro is that it can be time-consuming to learn and requires careful attention to detail when formatting outputs. Despite this, it is a powerful tool for creating professional-quality visualizations.
